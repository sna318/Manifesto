Legacy Russell

She/they. Black, female-identifying, femme, queer writer and curator born and raised in NYC. Executive Director & Chief Curator at The Kitchen. Raised in Saint Marks Place, right when the East Village in NYC was getting gentrified. LuvPunk12. Glitch Feminism was published in 2020, but the original paper commissioned by Rhizome, "Digital Dualism and the Glitch Feminism Manifesto," was published in 2012. This manifesto is born out of the highs and lows of Legacy's experiences as a queer Black femme, as well as the experiences of artists and activists who've glitched.

The Internet More Recently

  • 2012: the Obama administration championed fair use of user-generated content in opposing the Online Piracy Act and Protect Intellectual Property Act.
  • 2013: NSA was outed as surveilling people's digital communications.
  • 2015: Instagram surpasses Twitter in user count.
  • 2018: significant rise in Internet-enabled services.
  • 2019: 5G launched.
  • 2019-2020: height of the COVID pandemic.

Advocates for...

  • Embracing yourself as a glitch - reject the confines of gender and body
  • Embracing the multiplicity of being your online and offline self
  • Taking up space on the Internet
  • Unplugging if you need to - ignore texts, messages, ads
  • Not caring if your existence causes the Internet to short circuit
  • Defining who YOU are
  • Encrypting your presence on the Internet - you choose who gets to know the bits and pieces of your identity
  • Experimenting with your identity
  • Accepting and embracing slowness, malfunctions
  • Creating a mess and coding errors into machines that are designed and ruled by white patriarchy

Rejects...

  • The idea of IRL - your digital persona is very much real
  • The urge to shrink back and fit in
  • Being afraid of breaking something
  • The fixed idea of what a body looks like and how it functions
  • Self-categorization - don't use autofill, protect your data, don't tick off that box
  • The notion that the Internet is alienating - reclaim space for yourself
  • Electronic colonialism and mass consumerism
